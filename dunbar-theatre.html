About

A neighbourhood theatre since 1935

The Dunbar Theatre is a community landmark at 4555 Dunbar Street, one of the last remaining neighbourhood cinemas in Vancouver. Designed by Scottish-born architect David Colville and built by Carter-Halls-Aldinger Co., the theatre originally featured a striking Art Deco facade with Streamline Moderne details — a concrete blade sign with neon lighting, a wedge-shaped concrete marquee, and a black Vitrolite entryway with Chinese red lacquer doors.

Commissioned by film pioneer J. Howard Boothe through his Provincial Amusement Company, the Dunbar has screened films through the Depression, the war years, the television era, and the pandemic. Under current ownership since 1998, the theatre has been upgraded with digital and 3D projection while preserving its original pilasters, plaster cornice, coved ceiling, and stained wood wainscotting.

Today, it seats 350 across the main floor and mezzanine balcony. Wide, reclinable seats with big legroom. One screen. No booking fees. The best organic popcorn in the city.

History

Nine decades on Dunbar Street

Commissioned by film pioneer J. Howard Boothe. Designed by architect David Colville. Built to last.

1935
Opening night
Opens November 22, commissioned by Provincial Amusement Co. Ltd. (J. Howard Boothe). Art Deco facade with Streamline Moderne details: concrete blade sign, neon lighting, wedge-shaped marquee, black Vitrolite entryway, Chinese red lacquer doors. Built by Carter-Halls-Aldinger Co. One of 26 cinemas in Vancouver.
1941
First Odeon in Western Canada
Odeon Theatres of Canada purchases the Dunbar along with the Vogue, Plaza, and Paradise theatres on Granville Street. Boothe — a BC film pioneer acquainted with Charlie Chaplin — stays on as district manager until 1951.
1964
The renovation
An extensive renovation adds the north-side addition, removes the original Streamline Moderne facade elements, replaces the concrete blade sign with a read-o-graph letter board, and installs a curved metal canopy. The Vitrolite entryway is replaced with aluminum framing.
1987
Curtains close
The theatre closes on June 18. Final film: "The Believers." Briefly reopened by Famous Players in 1988 with "Three Men and a Baby" before closing again.
1998
A student's dream
Three university students, including Ken Charko, approach the property owner about a summer project. Charko becomes the permanent owner, upgrading seating, adding digital and 3D projection, and launching programs like Movies for Mommies.
2020
Surviving COVID
Against the odds, the Dunbar stays open through the pandemic — a testament to the neighbourhood's support for their last independent cinema.
Now
The last one standing
Vancouver's sole surviving full-time independent neighbourhood cinema. A planned heritage redevelopment will restore the original 1935 facade while building a new 5-storey mixed-use structure behind it — ensuring the Dunbar endures for another 90 years.

Should Dunbar offer these? A quick feasibility take.

Advance Ticket + Free Popcorn: This one's a no-brainer. A small popcorn costs the theatre roughly $0.25–0.40 in ingredients. At a $5.50 retail sticker, customers perceive huge value, but the actual cost is negligible. The real win is shifting ticket purchases online — that reduces box office staffing needs during rush, smooths capacity planning, and gets people to the concession counter (where they'll likely add a drink or candy). Every major chain does this in some form. Recommended: yes, launch immediately.

The Dunbar Pass at $249.99/year: At $4.81 per movie, the theatre gives up ~$7.18 per screening vs. walk-up price. But film rental costs for an independent cinema typically run 40–55% of ticket revenue, so the variable cost per seat is roughly $4.80–$6.60. At $4.81/ticket, the Pass roughly covers variable costs. The profit engine is concessions: Pass holders visit 52 times a year instead of the average 4–6, and the 10% concession discount still yields ~85%+ margins on popcorn and drinks. A single Pass holder buying a medium combo ($12) even half their visits generates ~$265/year in high-margin concession revenue. Plus: predictable recurring revenue, stronger community loyalty, and word-of-mouth. Risk: if every seat goes to Pass holders on a sold-out night, walk-up revenue drops. Mitigate with a one-ticket-per-week cap and no advance-booking priority on opening weekends. Recommended: yes, pilot with 200 memberships and evaluate after 6 months.
